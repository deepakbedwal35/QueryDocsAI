GNW specifies that activity recruiting fronto-\n9 \nparietal networks will elicit a subjective experience, while IIT is more concerned with the functional architecture \n0 \nof networks than with activity within it. Here, we argue that according to IIT mathematics, circuits converging on \n1 \nintegrative vs. convergent yet non-integrative neurons should support a greater degree of consciousness. We \n2 \ntest this hypothesis by analyzing a dataset of neuronal responses collected simultaneously from primary \n3 \nsomatosensory cortex (S1) and ventral premotor cortex (vPM) in non-human primates presented with auditory, \n4 \ntactile, and audio-tactile stimuli as they are progressively anesthetized with Propofol. We first describe the \n5 \nmultisensory (audio-tactile) characteristics of S1 and vPM neurons (mean and dispersion tendencies, as well \n6 \nas noise-correlations), and functionally label these neurons as convergent or integrative according to their \n7 \nspiking responses. Then, we characterize how these different pools of neurons behave as a function of \n8 \nconsciousness. At odds with the IIT mathematics, results suggest that convergent neurons more readily exhibit \n9 \nproperties of consciousness (neural complexity and noise correlation) and are more impacted during the loss \n0 \nof consciousness than integrative neurons. While in recent years a number of electrophysiological measures of \n0 \nconsciousness/awareness have been proposed [4-6], these tend to be more practical than principled, and \n1 \ngrounded more in the realm of engineering than neurobiology.  \n2 \n \n3 \nLacking a mechanistic account of consciousness, a number of theorists and researchers have started from \n4 \nempirical observations or phenomenological axioms to derive consciousness theories. A number of these \n5 \ntheories share many commonalities - as well as a number of practical and conceptual differences – as \n6 \nexemplified by two of the most prevailing and influential of these theories: Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW; \n7 \n[7, 8]) and Integrated Information Theory (IIT;[9-11], but see [12-17], for a number of other theories). \n8 \n \n9 \nThe GNW posits that an external stimulus will evoke a conscious experience if the associated neural \n0 \ninformation is widely distributed across distinct brain areas and networks – most prominently in the pre-frontal \n1 \ncortex [8]. Rendering sensory information globally available results in a coherent neural assembly of sustained \n2 \nactivity and is most readily indexed via “neural ignition”, the non-linear process whereby in unconscious states \n3 \nneuronal activation profiles remain encapsulated within their specialized subsystems, whereas in conscious \n4 \nexperiences these activation patterns are widely distributed (see [18] for experimental evidence and [19] for a \n5 \nrecent computational treatise). \n6 \n \n7 \nIn a similar manner, IIT is a systems-level theory of consciousness also postulating that complex and highly \n8 \ninterconnected neural networks support subjective experience [11, 20]. In contrast to GNW, however, the \n9 \nmathematics developed in hand with the IIT [9, 11] are argued to apply to all physical networks, and in turn IIT \n0 \nis arguably more focused on the architecture of networks rather than the activity within these.
