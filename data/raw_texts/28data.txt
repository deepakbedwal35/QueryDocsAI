Abstract 
6 
 
7 
Both the Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW) and Integrated Information Theory (IIT) posit that highly complex 
8 
and interconnected networks engender perceptual awareness. GNW specifies that activity recruiting fronto-
9 
parietal networks will elicit a subjective experience, while IIT is more concerned with the functional architecture 
0 
of networks than with activity within it. Here, we argue that according to IIT mathematics, circuits converging on 
1 
integrative vs. convergent yet non-integrative neurons should support a greater degree of consciousness. We 
2 
test this hypothesis by analyzing a dataset of neuronal responses collected simultaneously from primary 
3 
somatosensory cortex (S1) and ventral premotor cortex (vPM) in non-human primates presented with auditory, 
4 
tactile, and audio-tactile stimuli as they are progressively anesthetized with Propofol. We first describe the 
5 
multisensory (audio-tactile) characteristics of S1 and vPM neurons (mean and dispersion tendencies, as well 
6 
as noise-correlations), and functionally label these neurons as convergent or integrative according to their 
7 
spiking responses. Then, we characterize how these different pools of neurons behave as a function of 
8 
consciousness. At odds with the IIT mathematics, results suggest that convergent neurons more readily exhibit 
9 
properties of consciousness (neural complexity and noise correlation) and are more impacted during the loss 
0 
of consciousness than integrative neurons. Lastly, we provide support for the GNW by showing that neural 
1 
ignition (i.e., same trial co-activation of S1 and vPM) was more frequent in conscious than unconscious states. 
2 
Overall, we contrast GNW and IIT within the same single-unit activity dataset, and support the GNW.

Introduction 
5 
 
6 
Understanding the neural architecture enabling arousal or wakefulness (i.e., level or state of consciousness) 
7 
and conscious experience (i.e., content of consciousness) remains a central unanswered question in systems 
8 
neuroscience despite its profound clinical implications in coma, vegetative-state, minimal-consciousness, and 
9 
general anesthesia [1-3]. While in recent years a number of electrophysiological measures of 
0 
consciousness/awareness have been proposed [4-6], these tend to be more practical than principled, and 
1 
grounded more in the realm of engineering than neurobiology.  
2 
 
3 
Lacking a mechanistic account of consciousness, a number of theorists and researchers have started from 
4 
empirical observations or phenomenological axioms to derive consciousness theories. A number of these 
5 
theories share many commonalities - as well as a number of practical and conceptual differences – as 
6 
exemplified by two of the most prevailing and influential of these theories: Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW; 
7 
[7, 8]) and Integrated Information Theory (IIT;[9-11], but see [12-17], for a number of other theories). 
8 
 
9 
The GNW posits that an external stimulus will evoke a conscious experience if the associated neural 
0 
information is widely distributed across distinct brain areas and networks – most prominently in the pre-frontal 
1 
cortex [8]. Rendering sensory information globally available results in a coherent neural assembly of sustained 
2 
activity and is most readily indexed via “neural ignition”, the non-linear process whereby in unconscious states 
3 
neuronal activation profiles remain encapsulated within their specialized subsystems, whereas in conscious 
4 
experiences these activation patterns are widely distributed (see [18] for experimental evidence and [19] for a 
5 
recent computational treatise). 
6 
 
7 
In a similar manner, IIT is a systems-level theory of consciousness also postulating that complex and highly 
8 
interconnected neural networks support subjective experience [11, 20]. In contrast to GNW, however, the 
9 
mathematics developed in hand with the IIT [9, 11] are argued to apply to all physical networks, and in turn IIT 
0 
is arguably more focused on the architecture of networks rather than the activity within these. In more detail, 
1 
Tononi and colleagues argue that each conscious experience is highly informative, as it represents a particular 
2 
instance among a vast repertoire of potential experiences, and is highly unified, unable of being deconstructed 
3

into sub-experiences that are each independently perceived [21]. In turn, the IIT specifies that an organism 
4 
may support conscious experience if imbued with an information processing architecture that is capable of 
5 
supporting both high differentiation (i.e., large repertoire of possible states) and integration (i.e., strong 
6 
statistical dependencies between system components). 
7 
 
8 
Unfortunately, the neurophysiological data that bears directly on these theories is limited, in particular the IIT 
9 
given its computational overhead. Indeed, a strength of the IIT is that it explicitly generates a metric of 
0 
consciousness level; phi (Φ). This value, phi, can in principle be computed for any information processing 
1 
system, as long as the transition probability matrix between nodes of the system are known, and in essence is 
2 
proportional to the amount of information gained by knowing the state of all nodes within the system vs. having 
3 
access to a limited purview of the system (see [9] for more detail). Regretably, computing this measure in 
4 
complex biological systems is impossible from a practical standpoint due to its combinatorial search problem 
5 
(but see [22, 23] for interesting approaches circumventing current computing limitations).  
6 
 
7 
In an effort to provide empirical evidence germane to theories of consciousness, we propose here simple 
8 
neurophysiological benchmarks for consciousness as derived from the GNW and IIT, and test them empirically 
9 
in single unit recordings in non-human primates. Of note, we must emphasize that the predictions derived 
0 
below, are according to IIT mathematics and are logical consequence to IIT and GNW literature, yet are not 
1 
necessarily put forward explicitly by either IIT or GNW theorists. Further, these predictions bear on the nature 
2 
of neural processing according to these theories, and are mute regarding “what it feels like” [24] or the “Hard 
3 
Problem” [25] of consciousness. With these caveats in mind, first we formalize the role of multisensory neurons 
4 
that integrate information from multiple sensory modalities (operationalized as being driven by multisensory 
5 
stimulation than to unisensory stimulation, “AND” gates) vs. those that converge yet do not integrate 
6 
(operationalized as responding to multiple sensory modalities but not being further driven by multisensory 
7 
conditions, “XOR” gates = “OR” gates – “AND” gates; see [26] for an early characterization of multisensory 
8 
neurons as Boolean gates). Interestingly, IIT mathematics suggests that a simple 3-node network (e.g., 
9 
unisensory audio node, unisensory tactile node, and multisensory audio-tactile node) merging on an “AND” 
0 
gate bears a greater degree of integrated information than one converging on an “XOR” gate (Φ = 0.78 vs. Φ =

To probe these predictions we simultaneously record single units from the primary somatosensory cortex (S1) 
1 
and ventral pre-motor cortex (vPM) of non-human primates as they were presented with audio, tactile, or 
2 
audio-tactile stimuli. The monkeys were trained to report the presence of a stimulus (regardless of sensory 
3 
modality) via button press in order to determine their trial-to-trial alertness during propofol-induced loss of 
4 
consciousness (see [27]). We first characterize both the central (e.g., mean) and dispersion (e.g., variance) 
5 
tendencies of multisensory responses in S1 and vPM neurons under normal wakefulness, and based on these 
6 
responses, divide neurons into integrative or convergent categories. We then describe the impact of 
7 
anesthesia on neuronal ascription to these categories as the animals lose consciousness (testing prediction 
8 
#1) and the degree to which they exhibit two neurophysiological indices that vary with consciousness - 
9 
complexity [28] and noise correlations [29] (testing prediction #2). Finally, the fact that recordings were 
0 
performed simultaneously within a known microcircuit (S1 & vPM; [30]) allowed us to examine single trial 
1 
neural ignition as a function of consciousness (testing prediction #3).

6 
 
7

8 
293 single units in S1 (228 from Monkey E and 65 from Monkey H) and 140 single units in vPM (87 from 
9 
Monkey E and 53 from Monkey H) recorded across 26 sessions (16 in Monkey E and 10 in Monkey H). 
0

Firing Rates in S1 and vPM neurons 
8 
 
9 
Regarding the basic characterization of multisensory neurons in S1 and vPM, firing rates demonstrate 1) a 
0 
reliable response to stimulus onset (Figure 1, 1st and 3rd row; colored horizontal bars indicate evoked response 
1 
vs. baseline at p<0.01), and 2) a reduction in activity when monkeys were rendered unconscious, both with 
2 
regard to spontaneous (i.e., baseline,) and evoked activity (Figure 1, 1st and 3rd row; shaded area represents 
3 
the difference between evoked activity when animals were conscious and not at p<0.01). Further, as expected 
4 
given the known role of vPM in auditory processing [31], neurons in vPM, but not S1, generally responded to 
5 
auditory stimulation (interaction at p<0.01 between 60-210 ms post-stimuli onset).  
6 
 
 
7 
Fano Factors in S1 and vPM neurons 
8 
 
9 
Fano Factors (FF) were calculated to assess inter-trial response variability as a function of brain area, 
0 
stimulation type, and state of consciousness. It has been previously reported that FFs are larger in an 
1 
unconscious states (~2.2) than in conscious states [29]. Our results support a similar pattern, with FFs values 
2 
being on average 1.45 under unconscious conditions and 1.16 under conscious conditions. This pattern fits 
3 
nicely with the notion that conscious neural representations are more reproducible than unconscious ones [32]. 
4 
The results also show an interesting pattern of variability changes as a function of stimulus onset. Whereas 
5 
prior work has shown a reduction in variability upon stimulus onset [33], the current results illustrate larger 
6 
reductions in FF upon stimulus presentation in unconscious as opposed to conscious states (see SI for detail). 
7 
This observation illustrates that firing rates and FFs are not directly yoked to one another (see [29], for a similar 
8 
argument), and thus emphasizes the need to examine both central and dispersion tendencies in the response 
9 
profiles of these neural populations.

Discussion 
1 
 
2 
Detailing the neural mechanisms enabling wakefulness (i.e., consciousness-level) and conscious experiences 
3 
(i.e., consciousness-level) is a central question within systems neuroscience [7]. As such, several theoretical 
4 
frameworks have been put forth [7, 8, 11-17, 50]. Two of the most influential of these are Information 
5 
Integration Theory (IIT; [11, 50]) and Global Neuronal Workspace (GNW; [7, 8]). Unfortunately, these theories 
6 
have seldomly been tested neurophysiologically, and never within the same dataset. A series of concrete 
7 
predictions can be generated from these theories, and in the current work we sought to generate such 
8 
predictions and test them.   
9 
 
0 
Starting from IIT mathematics, a strong prediction that can be made is that as an organism transitions from 
1 
conscious to unconscious states, central integrative hubs (vs. convergent hubs) of neural networks should be 
2 
most impacted. Indeed, IIT states that the greater the information possessed by a network above and beyond 
3 
its constituent parts, the more conscious the system [11, 50]. Level of consciousness may be calculated and 
4 
represented as the variable phi (Φ). We demonstrate that within a simple three-node network, if the central 
5 
node is an integrative (“AND” gate) node as opposed to a convergent (“XOR” gate) node, the value of Φ triples 
6 
(see SI). In evaluating the neuronal data, we categorized neurons as either convergent or integrative and 
7 
examined which class was most impacted by propofol administration. The assumption here is that cross-modal 
8 
neurons in S1 and vPM receive information regarding the different senses from upstream areas, S1 and vPM 
9 
in turn being the central node composed of “AND” and “XOR” functionality. Of course, this is an over-simplified 
0 
biological neural network, but one that permits testing predictions derived from the IIT from a 
1 
neurophysiological perspective. Contrary to our IIT-derived predictions, convergent, as opposed to integrative, 
2 
neurons were most impacted by the administration of anesthesia. To further test predictions derived from IIT, 
3 
we reasoned that when organisms were conscious, integrative neurons should exhibit neurophysiological 
4 
properties of consciousness to a greater extent than convergent neurons (i.e., supporting lower phi-values). 
5 
The two measures chosen were Lempel-Ziv complexity and noise correlations, and we examined these as a 
6 
.

8

9

0

1 
complexity (PCI) and stimulus-evoked complexity is far from clear [51]). The study of noise correlations was 
2 
chosen as this measure has a stronger tradition within neurophysiology, and prior studies [29] have shown 
3 
substantial increases in noise correlations after administration of an opioid anesthetic. Here again, findings 
4 
indicated that convergent neurons most closely tracked the animals’ consciousness state. Taken together, the 
5 
findings of these neurophysiological analyses fail to provide strong empirical support for the IIT.  
6 
 
7 
One of the novel findings of the current study is that under propofol – a GABAA potentiator [52] – noise 
8 
correlations are approximately six-fold greater than during wakefulness. This finding is in line with previous 
9 
single unit recordings under a different anesthetic (opioid; [29]) and concordant with a recent graph theory 
0 
analysis of electrophysiological data showing that a change in local information processing efficiency – a 
1 
measure that changes with noise correlations - could differentiate between distinct levels of responsiveness 
2 
due to propofol administration [53].  
3 
 
4 
The second theory tested was GNW [7, 8]. In GNW the core concept is that during wakefulness a conscious 
5 
experience should result in neural ignition – the broadcasting of sensory evidence throughout the brain. 
6 
Concordant with the basic tenets of GNW, our results suggest that the co-activation of primary sensory areas 
7 
and higher-order areas on a single trial is more likely under conscious than unconscious conditions. 
8 
Importantly, the occurrence of this co-activation exceeded the expected values derived from the probability of 
9 
noting S1 and vPM activations alone. In addition to these co-activation findings, we analyzed firing rates in a 
0 
time-resolved fashion, which allowed us the opportunity to see whether firing rates to sensory stimulation 
1 
during consciousness were more sustained than during unconsciousness. As predicted by the GNW and well 
2 
established in electroencephalography and electrocorticography [54, 55, 56], neural activity was more 
3

5 
 
6 
In addition to providing empirical neurophysiological evidence relevant to two prevailing theories of 
7 
consciousness, our results make several novel contributions to the study of multisensory integration. First, to 
8 
our knowledge, this is the first report to detail that supra-additivity and enhancement indices are normally 
9 
distributed in both vPM and S1 of non-human primates (see [57], for a categorization of S1 neurons 
0 
demonstrating enhancement and supra-additivity in rats). Second, we observed a large number of neurons 
1 
exhibiting multisensory enhancement in S1 and vPM, yet very few that exhibited supra-additivity. These results 
2 
comport well with the known multisensory convergence in vPM [58, 59], but also represent the first evidence 
3 
that these neurons can integrate this information. Third, we detail the dispersion tendencies associated with 
4 
the firing patterns of (multi)sensory neurons in S1 and vPM. Variance in neuronal firing may be a result of a 
5 
variety of causes [33], both internal to the neuron or as a network property. Interestingly, while the Fano Factor 
6 
is likely impacted by both these sources, an elevation in noise correlations likely reflects a source of co-
7 
modulation. Thus, the current results demonstrating an increase in both Fano Factor and noise correlations 
8 
during unconsciousness suggests a dynamical system in which the firing patterns of individual neurons are 
9 
becoming more chaotic yet the population as a whole is more synchronously co-activated; an observation that 
0 
is in line with reports suggesting a potentiation of slow oscillations and a reduction of high-frequencies during 
1 
unawareness [27]. Finally, from the observations that conscious states are seemingly associated with low 
2 
noise correlations and that neurons showing multisensory convergence (as opposed to integration) more 
3 
faithfully track consciousness according to this metric, we reasoned that perhaps a high degree of noise 
4 
correlation is beneficial to multisensory integration. In fact, our results suggest a positive correlation between 
5 
the amount a neuron shares noise with its neighbors, and the degree to which it exhibits multisensory 
6 
integration. We find this result particularly interesting, as multisensory integration is a special form of 
7 
integration – a form that has minimal shared variance at the periphery, since information is transduced by 
8 
different sensory organs. This relationship between shared noise and greater multisensory integration may be 
9 
a result of larger dendritic arborizations in integrative neurons (see [60, 61]), and may represent the neural

In conclusion, we started from the IIT [11] and the GNW [7, 8] to derive neurophysiological predictions relating 
4 
to consciousness. We then leveraged multisensory neurons and circuits to functionally label neurons as 
5 
convergent and integrative, and used these categorical distinctions to test IIT and GNW-derived predictions. 
6 
The neurophysiological results generally support the GNW and not the IIT. However, important caveats exist. 
7 
First, it is possible that the predictions we generated according to the IIT represented a higher bar to clear than 
8 
those we generated from the GNW. Indeed, this is a strength of the IIT (i.e., making strong prediction) – and 
9 
thus future work should aim at continuing to translate theoretical postulates into concrete hypotheses, and 
0 
subsequently testing these hypotheses. Second, the IIT predictions derived and tested here represent the 
1 
simplest implementation and interpretation of the theory possible. Only three nodes were used, and only a 
2 
single node was changed in calculating different phi values. Further, we have assumed that single unit spiking 
3 
activity was a good approximation of the behavior of “nodes” within the IIT. This is far from trivial, as for 
4 
example, in the IIT formalism nodes are either “on” or “off”, yet real neurons can show graded levels of activity. 
5 
Third, while simultaneously recording from S1 and vPM lends nicely to testing the GNW – given their known 
6 
micro-circuitry [30, 31] – it may be argued that these areas are not ideal for testing the IIT. Indeed, Koch and 
7 
colleagues [65], researchers supporting the IIT, recently suggest that anatomical correlates of consciousness 
8 
are primarily localized to a “posterior hot zone”. Thus, in the future it may be interesting to test similar ideas to 
9 
those presented here in the posterior parietal cortex (but see [66] for arguments suggesting that the neural 
0 
correlates of consciousness are in the “front” of the brain). Beyond specific objections related to the IIT, it must 
1 
be emphasized that the results reported here are exclusive for propofol anesthesia. Hence, generalization of 
2 
these results to the broader domain of consciousness must be done with caution. Future work may aim at 
3 
replicating the above-described findings during the administration of several distinct anesthetics – the union of 
4 
effects safely being able to be ascribed to consciousness. Further, it must be acknowledged that IIT is primarily 
5 
a theory of consciousness-level, while GNW primarily focuses of consciousness-content. These different 
6 
aspects of consciousness are closely related (as one does not consciously perceive the external environment if 
7 
in an unconscious state), but dissociable. Here both aspects were conflated (i.e., it is assumed animals did not 
8

Firing Rate and Fano Factor 
7 
 
8 

 
31
Both central and dispersion tendencies of single-unit spiking activity in S1 and vPM were quantified as a 
9 
function of stimulus modality as it is well-established that mean firing rates alone do not fully characterize the 
0 
properties of neural activity [33]. Regarding firing rates, spikes were first binned in 1ms intervals, and epochs 
1 
were centered on stimuli onset, ranging from 2000ms prior to stimuli onset (i.e., -2000ms), to 2000ms after 
2 
stimuli onset. Subsequently spike counts were effectuated within a 100ms window, between -500ms and 
3 
1000ms, and in steps of 10ms. It must be noted that this analysis essentially low-passes time, and hence the 
4 
exact timing of reported effects should not be emphasized. Analyses of firing rates were conducted both on 
5 
baseline-corrected and non-corrected rates. The contrast of non-corrected rates allows for determining the 
6 
impact of propofol on baseline firing, while the analysis on baseline-corrected rates allows specifically querying 
7 
the evoked-responses to stimuli onset. That is, for the baseline-corrected rates, every spike count function was 
8 
centered along the y-axis (i.e., spikes/s) to zero according to their own baseline firing (-500 to 0ms post-stimuli 
9 
onset). In this manner, positive deviations from 0 indicate an increased in firing rate, while negative deflections 
0 
indicate a silencing in spiking activity post-stimuli onset with respect to baseline. Spike counts were first 
1 
averaged within a cell and across trials, and subsequently across neurons. In terms of statistical analyses, as 
2 
the temporal dynamics of spiking activity was of interest, in particular within the GNW theory [7, 8] emphasizing 
3 
sustained activity in aware and not unaware states, we conducted a time-resolved (at each 10ms time-point, 
4 
151 in total) 2 (State; Aware vs. Unaware) x 2 (Area; S1 vs. vPM) x 4 (Stimulation; AT, T, A, N) independent 
5 
samples analysis of variance (ANOVA). As spiking rates were not normally distributed (i.e., presence of a true 
6 
floor, in that negative spikes are not possible), the ANOVAs for non-baseline corrected rates were conducted 
7 
on log-transformed data. On the other hand, the subtraction of evoked activity to baseline activity did yield 
8 
normal distributions, and hence this data is analyzed without log-transform. The inter-dependence of 
9 
observations is difficult to ascertain within a neural network composed of neurons whose precise connections 
0 
are unknown, and thus independent as opposed to dependent ANOVAs were conducted in order to adopt the 
1 
most conservative approach (i.e., within-samples ANOVAs are statistically stronger than between-samples 
2 
analyses). Similarly, in order to protect against Type I error (i.e., false positives) significant effects were only 
3 
considered at α < 0.01 for at least 3 consecutive windows (i.e., 30 time-points; see [44], for a similar approach 
4 
with time-series data).

Regarding the inter-trial variance in evoked responses associated with the distinct states of consciousness, 
7 
stimuli modalities, and brain areas, fano factors (i.e., ratio of spike-count variance to spike-count mean) were 
8 
calculated [70]. Indeed, repeated trials do not yield identical responses, and this variance is associated both 
9 
with cellular and molecular processes involved in spike generation at the axon hillock (e.g., refractory periods) 
0 
and network-level properties [63]. Conveniently the neuron-specific variance is largely consiered to be well 
1 
accounted by a Poisson point process (i.e., mean and variance scale), and hence a fano factor of 1 [33, 64]. 
2 
Fano factors in excess of 1, thus, may be considered to index variability that is associated with network-level 
3 
properties and this variability is typically reduced at stimuli onset. Here, therefore, we report time-resolved fano 
4 
factor both corrected for baseline (in order to examine putative network-level decreases in inter-trial variability 
5 
as a function of stimuli onset, awareness state and sensory modality), and not corrected for baseline (in order 
6 
to assess basal cell-specific and network level inter-trial variability as a function of awareness state). Statistical 
7 
analysis is conducted as described above for firing rates.

Bifurcation Into Convergence and Integration 
3 
 
4 
Modeling results based on the IIT specify that a network converging on a neuron that integrates information, as 
5 
opposed to responding indiscriminately, ought to support a greater degree to consciousness. Hence, here we 
6 
aim at testing two predictions that may follow from the IIT; i) as an organism falls into unconsciousness, the 
7 
neurons that are most impacted are those that integrate information (i.e., putatively anesthetics act on these 
8 
neurons preferentially), and ii) neurons that integrate information exhibit the properties of consciousness when 
9 
the organism is conscious. To test these predictions, we divide our population of neurons into those that 
0 
integrate vs. converge (Figure 4 and beyond). However, initially we simply describe the proportion of neurons 
1 
that fit within each category (Figure 5) in a non-mutually exclusive fashion. A neuron that converges 
2 
information is defined as a neuron that on average (i.e., across trials) responds – spike count from 0 to 500ms 
3 
- to both unisensory auditory and tactile information beyond its baseline firing rate (-500ms to 0ms) plus 2 
4 
standard deviations. That is, in order to qualify as convergent, the spiking count of a neuron to AT stimulation 
5 
does not need to be examined. On the other hand, a neuron that integrates information is defined as a neuron 
6 
that is most readily driven by the simultaneous presence of A and T information. Thus, neurons that respond to 
7 
AT stimulation (as defined above) and do so to a greater degree than their maximal unisensory response (i.e., 
8 
enhancement index above 1) were initially classified as integrative. Importantly, beyond Figure 3 (e.g., to 
9 
categorize the fate of neurons when the animal becomes unconscious and quantify neural complexity, noise 
0 
correlations, and neural ignition) two mutually exclusive classes are created. Neurons that respond 
1 
indiscriminately to sensory stimulation and not preferentially to multisensory vs. unisensory presentations are 
2 
classified as convergent, while those that exhibit multisensory enhancement without being considered 
3 
convergent are taken to integrate information. Given the initial number of neurons in S1 and vPM, this 
4 
bifurcation yielded a sufficient quantity of neurons exclusively categorized as convergent (N = 125) and 
5 

 
34
integrative (N = 64) in S1, but not in vPM (convergent, N = 61; integrative, N = 8) – thus, for the analyses 
6 
specifically probing the difference between convergent and integrative neurons, analyses are restricted to S1. 
7 
Further, given the heterogeneity of neuron’s spike trains (see Figures 1-3) for Figure 3 and beyond we 
8 
considered a neuron as fitting within a particular category (e.g., A, T, AT convergent, AT integrative) if at some 
9 
point between 0ms and 1000ms post-stimuli onset they met the particular criteria for at least 50 consecutive 
0 
ms.   
1 
 
2 
Equally of note, in Figure 3 neurons that are labeled to integrate auditory and tactile information (purple, 
3 
orange, and green) are not first indexed for their unisensory responses. That is, while in S1, 49% of neurons 
4 
are classified as responding to a greater extent to AT stimulation than to the maximum unisensory stimulation, 
5 
this latter unisensory response is not necessarily different from baseline activity. We consider this approach 
6 
appropriate within the current aim of leveraging multisensory responses in querying consciousness theories, 
7 
but it must be highlighted that multisensory enhancement may be more strictly considered to apply only when 
8 
tactile, auditory, and audiotactile responses are different from baseline, and the latter responses is greater than 
9 
the maximal of the former two [27]. Indeed, the categorization here is more in line with the recent emphasis 
0 
within the study of multisensory integration to index covert multisensory processes [36], in particular within 
1 
classically considered primary sensory areas [74], than with the original description of multisensory integration 
2

Lempel-Ziv Complexity 
5 
 
6 
Categorizing the complexity of neural representations – operationalized as the number of distinct patterns 
7 
present in data – has become of increasing popularity as of late (e.g., [11; 33]) in particular due to its ability to 
8 
differentiate between states of consciousness given scalp electrophysiological data [6] and the belief that 
9 
complexity is at least indirectly related to functional differentiation/integration, paramount notions with the IIT 
0 
[11]. In order to quantify neural complexity, here we measure the Lempel-Ziv (LZ) complexity [28] associated 
1 
with each spike train evoked as a consequence of AT, T, A, or N trials, and as a function of the animals’ 
2 
consciousness state. LZ complexity measures the approximate quantity of non-redundant information 
3 
contained within a string by estimating the minimal size of the “vocabulary” necessary to describe the entirety 
4 

 
35
of the information contained within the string in a lossless manner [28]. That is, it is a lossless compression 
5 
algorithm (routinely used in ZIP files and TIFF images), and it is utilized to measure the number of distinct 
6 
patterns in symbolic sequences, in particular within binary signals. LZ is impacted by the overall entropy within 
7 
a signal [45]; i.e., a binary string composed almost exclusively of ‘0’ will not have a high LZ, not due to the 
8 
arrangement of those ‘1’, but simply because there are not many of them). Thus, here, to equate entropy 
9 
across conditions we first converted spike trains into a continuous measure by convolving each trial with a 
0 
Gaussian kernel with σ=50ms, and then binarized each time-point within this trial by assigning a ‘1’ to time-
1 
points above the trial mean, and ‘0’ to time-points below the trial mean. Next, LZ was computed (28) in 
2 
MATLAB within a sliding window moving between -500ms and 750ms post-stimuli onset, a length of 100ms, 
3 
and step size of 50ms. Lastly, the same procedure was executed while randomly shuffling the binary sequence 
4 
before calculating LZ. This shuffled LZ time-series represents a theoretical upper bound (i.e., random data has 
5 
a higher LZ) and was used to normalize the calculated LZ from the non-shuffled data. Hence, a normalized LZ 
6 
of 1 indicates ‘as complex as random noise’, while lower values indicate the presence of structure in the data 
7 
(see [43, 44], for a similar approach). Statistical analysis largely followed that of firing rates and fano factors, 
8 
which exception that data were never log-transformed as they were normally distributed. Analysis was 
9 
effectuated both on baseline-corrected values, in order to compare the negative deflection present during 
0 
stimulus onset (see [43] for a similar findings) and most importantly, on non-corrected values, in order to 
1 
examine the basal complexity in spiking activity as a function of consciousness and whether neurons were 
2 
categorized as convergent or integrative.

Noise Correlations 
5 
 
6 
While LZ complexity is arguably the most often utilized measure within the IIT framework [11], it is not a 
7 
traditional measure within neurophysiology. Thus, we sought to further probe the properties of convergent and 
8 
integrative neurons – and their correspondence with the alteration in the particular measure as a function of 
9 
consciousness state – with a neurophysiological measure that is well established to alter with consciousness 
0 
state. Noise correlations [29] express the amount of covariability in the trial-to-trial fluctuations of responses of 
1 
two neurons to repeated presentations of the same stimuli, are central to questions of coding accuracy and 
2 
efficiency [75], and are well-established to be altered by consciousness state [29]. Thus, this measure was 
3 

 
36
computed both in S1 and vPM neurons, as a function of consciousness state and stimuli modality. Noise 
4 
correlations where computed as the Pearson correlation between all pairs of neurons recorded simultaneously 
5 
within the same session (see [29] for a similar approach). Spike counts were effectuated for each trial on the 
6 
500ms immediately following stimuli presentation (defined above as the average time-period of neural 
7 
response, and in concert with [29]. We considered the noise correlation for a particular neuron it’s average 
8 
correlation with all other neurons recorded in the same session.

Neural Ignition 
1 
 
2 
The GNW model points to the late amplification of relevant sensory activity, long-distance cortico-cortical 
3 
synchronization at beta and gamma frequencies, and ignition of large-scale fronto-parietal networks as neural 
4 
measures of consciousness [8]. To test this prediction, we query at the single trial level whether sensory 
5 
stimulation leads to co-activation of both primary sensory areas (i.e., S1) and frontal regions (i.e., vPM) more 
6 
commonly during conscious than unconscious states. For each neuron (both in S1 and vPM) we specify a 
7 
threshold benchmarking reliable neural activity as the average spike count between -500 and 0 ms post-stimuli 
8 
onset plus 2 standard deviations. Similarly, the neural response is considered to be the spike-count between 0 
9 
and 500 post-stimuli onset. Then, iteratively we pick a neuron from S1 and a neuron from vPM and query 
0 
whether on a particular trial did neither area respond, did solely S1 respond, did solely vPM respond, or did 
1 
both S1 and vPM respond. A particular S1 neuron is subsequently paired with all neurons in vPM recorded 
2 
during the same session, and finally it’s mean activation patterns (e.g., S1 and vPM active, vPM active, S1 
3 
active, or none) as a function of consciousness state and sensory stimulation are quantified. The same 
4 
procedure is applied to vPM neurons. It must be highlighted that routinely mean firing rates are largely driven 
5 
by strong responses in a few trials [33], for example), and hence demanding a response within a particular trial 
6 
to exceed baseline plus 2 standard deviations is a conservative approach yielding a great number of no-
7 
response trials. Nonparametric statistics are used in this analysis as data did not confirm to the assumptions 
8 
made by parametric inference statistics.

Formalizing the Role of Multisensory Integration in Consciousness 
4 
 
5 
To formally ascertain the putative role multisensory integrative (vs. convergent) neurons within a network in 
6 
bearing consciousness (according to the IIT), we built two biologically inspired simple neural networks (Figure 
7 
S2A). These networks each have 3 nodes, two of which may be considered analogous to unisensory areas 
8 
(nodes A and B) and a third (node C), which receives projections from the unisensory areas and may be 
9 
considered analogous to a multisensory area. As is well established in biological systems, the multisensory 
0 
area equally projected back to unisensory areas (Bizley et al., 2007, Cappe et al., 2009, Ghazanfar and 
1 
Schroeder, 2006). The two networks were identical with exception that for one network (Figure S2A, left panel) 
2 
node C was an “XOR” gate, while for the other it was an “AND” gate (Figure S2A, right panel). The XOR gate 
3 
results in a logical “true” (or ‘1’/ ‘HIGH’) when the number of true inputs is odd. In this case, given the system 
4 
architecture, the node C would be active if on the previous time step one and only one of gates A or B was 
5 
active. Thus, node C can in principle be active following activity in either node A or B, but importantly does not 
6 
respond preferentially when both are active. On the other hand, the other network, where node C is an “AND” 
7 
gate, responds exclusively when on the precedent time-step both gates A and B were active. That is, this gate 
8 
most faithfully mimics the behavior of integrative multisensory neurons that may or may not overtly respond 
9 
indiscriminately to distinct sensory inputs, but importantly are most driven by spatio-temporally coincident 
0 
multisensory inputs. Hence, the network formed with an XOR gate (Figure S2A, left) instantiates a network with 
1 
neurons that are indiscriminant to the nature of sensory input, while in contrast the network formed with an 
2 
AND gate (Figure S2A, right) instantiates a network with neurons that integrate sensory information, i.e., 
3 
responds non-linearly to the addition of sensory stimuli from distinct modalities (Stein and Stanford, 2008, 
4 
Wallace et al., 2006). The architecture of these systems dictates the composition of transition probability 
5 
matrices (TPMs), which guides transitions between states (i.e., neurons that are ‘active’ at different time-
6 
points). In Figure S2B these TPMs have been depicted (left and right respectively for the multisensory 
7 
convergent and multisensory integrative systems) and highlighted for their distinctive features. Namely, in the
