SUMMARY 
Retrospective attention, directed by cues after stimulus offset, enhances the perception 
of threshold-level Gabor patches. However, the neural mechanisms and the dependence 
on cue awareness and target reporting remain unclear. Using EEG in both report and no-
report paradigms with visible and invisible post-cues, we found that orientation 
discrimination improved with retro-cues regardless of cue visibility. Under invisible 
conditions, retro-cueing effects were weaker, limited to a stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA) 
of 66.67 ms, and associated with the N2pc component. In contrast, visible retro-cueing 
persisted at a 100 ms SOA, eliciting strong P3b and weak VAN in report condition. 
Multivariate pattern analysis revealed that in report conditions, neural processing of cued 
targets was earlier, stronger, and more generalized in fronto-parietal regions, whereas in 
no-report conditions, decoding was confined to parieto-occipital areas. These findings 
indicate that retrospective attention can enhance visual perception independently of cue 
awareness and target reporting, contributing to ongoing theories of consciousness. 
 
 
INTRODUCTION 
Visual perception is a complex process influenced by various factors, including attention. An 
intriguing question in visual perception and consciousness research is whether cues presented 
after the offset of a visual stimulus can induce visual reactivation and retrospectively influence our 
conscious perception of the stimulus
1,2. This phenomenon, known as the "retrospective attention 
effect," has significant implications for our understanding of consciousness and attention. 
Recent experiments have confirmed the existence of the retrospective attention effect
3-5. These 
studies have demonstrated that retro-cueing attention can retrospectively recover sensory traces 
that are about to disappear from consciousness, suggesting that retrospective attention acts as a 
gatekeeper mediating conscious perception 
5. Furthermore, a study by Thibault et al.
4, showed that 
directing exogenous attention to the target location after its presentation significantly improved

attention effect stems from working memory rather than a perceptual effect. 
However, the interpretation of these findings remains controversial. Some researchers argue that 
the retro-cueing effect is confounded by various cognitive processes, such as target attention, 
decision-making and motor functions
6,7,18,25-30. They contend that results at the behavioral level 
alone cannot adequately distinguish these processes. Adding to this complexity, a recent study by 
Stein
7 reported that a local visual interaction between the target and cue is sufficient to trigger 
consciousness of the target, revealing a new type of retrospective attention effect mediated by 
sensory (non-attentional) mechanisms. This suggests that exogenous attention may not be the 
only mechanism contributing to the retrospective attention effect. 
While robust effects of post-cue attention have been found in behavioral experiments
4,7,8, the 
neural mechanisms underlying these effects remain largely unknown. This gap in our 
understanding is particularly significant given the intricate connections between attention and 
consciousness identified in previous studies
9,10. Theories of consciousness, such as the Recurrent 
Processing Theory (RPT) and the Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)
9,10, provide 
frameworks for understanding these connections but have not been specifically applied to the 
retro-perception effect. 
To address these open questions and distinguish between different types of retro-perception 
effects, we designed three new experiments incorporating two key paradigms: 
The Backward Masking Paradigm
14,15: This paradigm can generate subliminal cues, thereby 
blocking top-down information transmission pathways and allowing us to isolate the effects of 
local visual interactions. 
The No-Report Paradigm
11-13: In this paradigm, participants do not make judgments about their 
perceptual experience, minimizing the neural correlates of post-perceptual processing
11. 
Additionally, we incorporated EEG recordings and multivariate pattern analysis to investigate the 
neural correlates associated with retro-perception effects. Specifically, we focused on ERP 
components such as N2pc and P3b
14 to investigate the relationship between consciousness and 
attention. 
By combining these paradigms, we aim to achieve two primary objectives: Dissociate the Two 
Types of Retro-Perception Effects: One based on local visual sensory interaction mechanisms and 
the other on attention mechanisms. Distinguish Conscious Perception from Motor Activity: 
Ensuring that the observed retrospective attention effect indeed enhances participants' conscious 
perception of the target at the neural level.  
We hypothesize that: The Backward Masking Paradigm Will Not Disrupt Sensory-Mediated Retro-
Perception Effects
15. Post-Cue Attention Will Enhance Neural Markers of Conscious Perception, 
even in the absence of explicit reporting, demonstrating its robustness beyond task-related 
cognitive processes. 
This study contributes to the field by providing the first neural evidence for the mechanisms 
underlying the retrospective attention effect, potentially reconciling conflicting accounts in the 
literature. Our findings will have implications for theories of consciousness and attention, offering 
new insights into the temporal dynamics of conscious perception and the role of post-perceptual 
processing in awareness. Moreover, by demonstrating the persistence of the post-cue effect in a 
no-report paradigm, we aim to establish its fundamental role in shaping conscious perception, 
independent of explicit report.

RESULTS 
Experiment 1 
We assessed whether post-cue attention
5 could influence the perception of a single stimulus at 
threshold in an unconscious exogenous attention paradigm
16. In this paradigm, the attentional cue 
provided no information on stimulus location, minimizing its influence on decision-making or 
other post-perceptual processes. The target stimulus was a single low-contrast Gabor patch of 
random orientation, which could be flashed randomly within one of two circles to the left or right 
of central fixation (Figure 1A). Participants were required to report the target’s orientation. Target 
contrast was individually calibrated to reach 75% accuracy without cueing. 
During the experiment, attention was randomly cued to the left or right by a brief dimming of one 
circle. Importantly, the dimming prevented masking of the target, unlike more conventional 
attentional cues such as thickening or abrupt onset of a shape near the target’s location. This cue 
appeared either before (pre-cue) or after (post-cue) the target. Following the cue or target, an 
83.33 ms mask or fixation period was presented for the invisible and visible conditions, respectively. 
At the end of each trial, a response cue (thickening of one side of the fixation cross) indicated the 
target’s side, eliminating location uncertainty. Subsequently, a second response screen appeared, 
displaying a dimming cross at the fixation point where participants reported the position of the 
cue in a two-alternative forced choice (2AFC) task. Twenty-four volunteers participated in this 
experiment. 
When no mask stimulus was presented (visible cue condition), whether the cue appeared before 
or after the target, our results replicated the typical time course of pre-cue and post-cue 
exogenous attention (Figure 2): congruent cues—same side as the target—improved orientation 
accuracy relative to incongruent cues—opposite side [ANOVA with factors congruency (2) × 
stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA) (3); congruency: F(1,23) = 111.64, p < 0.001]. Critically, congruent 
cues presented after the target also substantially improved orientation discrimination (Figure 2). 
Post-hoc comparisons with BONFERRONI correction indicated significant differences between 
valid and invalid cues in all three SOA conditions (-116.67 ms, corrected p < 0.001; +66.67 ms, 
corrected p < 0.001; +100 ms, corrected p < 0.001). This strong effect persisted 100 ms after the 
target, mainly due to the benefit of congruent cueing, which improved performance compared to 
75% accuracy in the absence of cueing. 
When a mask stimulus was presented, although the pre-cue and post-cue exogenous attention 
effects weakened, congruent cues still improved orientation accuracy relative to incongruent cues 
[ANOVA with factors congruency (2) × stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA) (3); congruency: F(1,23) 
= 16.33, p = 0.001]. Post-hoc comparisons with BONFERRONI correction indicated significant 
differences between valid and invalid cues in two SOA conditions (-116.67 ms, corrected p = 0.022; 
+66.67 ms, corrected p = 0.015), but not in the +100 ms condition (corrected p = 0.257) (Figure 
2). 
In conclusion, these results show that when a single stimulus is presented, directing attention to 
its location after its offset can still have a substantial effect on our ability to report a simple feature 
of this stimulus, such as orientation, even when the cue location is invisible. This indicates that 
post-cue attention likely reflects a local sensory interaction between the cue and target, rather 
than post-perceptual processes. Interestingly, we found that the post-cue attention effect 
vanished when the SOA between the target and the cue reached +100ms in the invisible cue 
condition. This suggests that the post-cue attention effect is highly time-sensitive under

Experiment 2 
Behavior Results 
This second experiment, including 23 new volunteers, was similar to the previous one except that 
the SOA between the target and the cue was fixed at 66.67 ms; a Nocue condition was added to 
the cue presentation; and only the mask stimulus was presented, thereby maintaining only the 
invisible condition (Figure 3). Results on the objective orientation discrimination task replicated the 
results of the first experiment (Figure 3) [congruency: F(1,20) = 5.66, p = 0.011]. Post-hoc 
comparisons with BONFERRONI correction indicated significant differences between valid and 
invalid cues (corrected p = 0.002), but not between valid and nocue condition (corrected p = 
0.169). 
 
EEG Results 
The N2pc in the report and no-report conditions in experiment 2 
Results from the N2pc analysis are shown in Figure 4. N2pc amplitudes significantly differed 
between the contralateral and ipsilateral electrodes across the three congruency conditions 
[ANOVA with factors congruency (valid/invalid/nocue, 3) × electrodes (ipsilateral/contralateral, 2): 
F(2, 20) = 2.264, p = 0.129; electrodes: F(1, 22) = 30.39, p < 0.001]. Post-hoc comparisons with 
BONFERRONI correction indicated significant differences between contralateral and ipsilateral 
electrodes in all three congruency conditions (Figure 4A, valid, corrected p < 0.001; Figure 4A, 
invalid, corrected p = 0.006; Figure 4B, nocue, corrected p < 0.001). N2pc difference wave 
amplitudes (contralateral minus ipsilateral) significantly differed between the valid, nocue and 
invalid conditions [ANOVA with factors congruency (valid/invalid/nocue, 3): F(2, 20) = 14.76, p < 
0.001]. Post-hoc comparisons with BONFERRONI correction indicated significant differences not 
only between valid and invalid conditions but not between valid and nocue conditions (valid versus 
invalid, corrected p = 0.025; valid versus nocue, corrected p = 0.231; invalid versus nocue, 
corrected p = 0.029). The results showed that even under conditions where the cue was invisible, 
post-cue attention could still modulate the classic attention-related ERP component-N2pc.

Experiment 3 
Behavior results 
This third experiment, involving 20 new participants, was similar to the first experiment but with 
several key modifications. The stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA) between the target and the cue 
was fixed at 66.67 ms. Only the fixation stimulus was presented, maintaining the visible condition. 
Two circles were positioned above and below the fixation point, with the Gabor patch and the cue

and high contrast conditions (corrected p = 0.079).

EEG Results 
The P3b in the report and no-report conditions in experiment3 
The results from the P3b analysis (see Methods) are shown in Figure 7. In the report condition, 
we observed a post-cueing effect in both high (100%) and medium (75%) contrast conditions for 
the P3b (medium: valid vs. invalid, t(19) = 20.131; P = 0.001 and high: valid vs. invalid, t(19) = 
4.77; P = 0.046; Figure 7A), indicating that post-cueing attention can enhance visual perception. 
There was no difference in P3b amplitude for the two lowest contrast conditions (valid vs. invalid, 
t(19) = 0.252; P = 0.623), consistent with previous behavioral studies.  
Most importantly, consistent with previous studies
11,13, we found no significant difference in P3b 
amplitude in the no-report condition (high: valid vs. invalid, t(19) = 2.516; P = 0.129; medium: 
valid vs. invalid, t(19) = 0.344; P = 0.565; low: valid vs. invalid, t(19) = 0.001; P = 0.882; Figure 7B). 
Interestingly, in the report task, when plotting the average amplitudes from all electrodes used 
for the P3b across all six conditions (low-invalid, low-valid, medium-invalid, medium-valid, 
high-invalid, high-valid), we observed a non-linear, bifurcated-like increase in amplitudes
12,13,17. 
This pattern is considered a potential correlate of conscious processing, replicating prior work
17. 
However, while we ensured that the three contrast levels were controlled in a linear manner, we 
could not guarantee that the different attention modulations (valid, invalid) formed a linear 
pattern. As a result, we did not conduct further in-depth analysis on this particular aspect.

The VAN in the report and no-report conditions in experiment3 
The “visual awareness negativity” (VAN)
18,19 is defined as a relatively more negative ERP signal for 
seen compared to unseen stimuli, with a bilateral posterior distribution, roughly in the time window 
between 150-300 ms. Results from the VAN analysis are shown in Figure 8. In the report condition, 
we observed a post-cueing effect in the VAN only at high (100%) contrast (high: valid vs. invalid, 
t(19) = 9.149; P = 0.009; medium: valid vs. invalid, t(19) = 0.066; P = 0.801; low: valid vs. invalid, 
t(19) = 0.516; P = 0.484; Figure 8A). Interestingly, the VAN pattern is exactly the opposite of the 
P3b, meaning the VAN amplitude in the invalid condition is more negative than in the valid 
condition. Similar with the P3b pattern, we found no difference in the VAN between valid and 
invalid in the no-report condition. (high: invalid vs. valid, t(19) = 1.251; P = 0.277; medium: invalid 
vs. valid, t(19) = 0.023; P = 0.882; low: invalid vs. valid, t(19) = 0.001; P = 0.981; Figure 8B).

Multivariate Analysis Results 
We trained linear classifiers for all 62 electrodes to distinguish between target and no-target 
conditions, using the amplitude across electrodes as features. Classifier performance was 
evaluated using the area under the curve (AUC) of the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) 
curve. 
 
Decoding Time Courses in the report condition and no-report condition in experiment3 
However, the temporal profiles of decoding performance varied across different contrast and 
congruency conditions (Figures 9A, 9B). In the report task, significant clusters emerged earlier after 
stimulus onset in the valid condition compared to the invalid condition for all three contrast 
conditions (all pcluster < 0.05, shown by different color horizontal bars in Figure 9A top). The temporal 
profiles of difference AUC (valid-invalid) revealed that significant clusters emerged around 400ms 
after stimulus onset and ended around 500ms in both high and medium contrast conditions 
(Figure 9A bottom). 
In the no-report task, significant clusters emerged only 300ms after stimulus onset in the high 
contrast-valid condition, but not in the other five conditions (all pcluster < 0.05, shown by different 
color horizontal bars in Figure 9B top). The temporal profiles of difference AUC (valid-invalid) 
showed that significant clusters emerged around 400ms and ended around 700ms after stimulus 
onset in only high contrast conditions (Figure 9B bottom).

Temporal Generalization Analysis in the Report condition and No-report condition in 
experiment3 
These findings demonstrate that the retro-cue effect can improve EEG decoding performance for 
the target Gabor patch and advance its decoding time, regardless of report or no-report task 
conditions. To test the temporal generalization performance of the retro-cue effect, we applied 
the temporal generalization method. In this approach, classifiers are trained on data from each 
time point separately but tested on all time points, resulting in a temporal generalization matrix 
(TGM; target present vs. target absent: Supplement Figure). Successful decoding between time 
points (off-diagonal decoding) indicates that the direction in state space discriminating between 
target and no-target conditions remains stable over time. 
The Temporal Generalization Analysis results revealed a pattern similar to the Decoding Time 
Courses. In the report task, we found strong evidence of a metastable representation associated 
with conscious perception, indicated by late square-shaped patterns in the temporal 
generalization matrix (Supplement Figure) in all conditions except the lowest contrast-invalid 
condition. These late square-shaped patterns
13,23-28 were stronger in the valid condition compared 
to the invalid condition. In the no-report condition, the late square-shaped patterns only occurred 
in the high contrast-valid condition (Supplement Figure). 
As with the temporal decoding analysis, we created a similar difference-AUC TGM matrix. In the 
report task, significant clusters emerged around 400ms after stimulus onset and ended around 
500ms in both high and medium contrast conditions (Supplement Figure, all pcluster < 0.05). In the 
no-report task, significant clusters emerged around 400ms and ended around 700ms after 
stimulus onset in only high contrast conditions (Supplement Figure, all pcluster < 0.05). 
Interestingly, in both analyses, the no-report high contrast condition contributed the longest 
significant time courses in the difference-AUC analysis, indicating an even longer-lasting retro-
cueing perception effect.

DISCUSSION 
The aim of this study was to dissociate two types of retrospective attention effects: those based 
on local visual sensory interaction mechanisms and those rooted in attention mechanisms. 
Additionally, we sought to distinguish between conscious perception and motor activity to confirm 
that the observed retro-cueing effect indeed enhances participants' conscious perception of the 
target at the neural level. To address these objectives, we conducted three experiments. In line 
with previous research by Sergent et al.
8, participants were asked to report a single target Gabor 
patch shortly after its presentation (within 1.5 seconds), ensuring no ambiguity regarding its 
location. 
 
The N2pc is associated with the subliminal retro-cueing effect. 
In our first experiment, we successfully replicated Sergent et al.'s
8 findings using a visible cue 
condition and observed a retro-cueing attention effect in the orientation discrimination task at a 
66.7 ms target-cue stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA). Notably, this effect persisted even with 
invisible cues. Our second experiment, utilizing EEG recordings, further validated these findings. 
Analysis of the classic attentional ERP component N2pc revealed significant differences between 
valid, no-cue, and invalid conditions. This neural signature demonstrates that subliminal cues can 
enhance exogenous attention toward the target. Additionally, by manipulating mask intensity, we 
successfully dissociated two types of retrospective attention effects, aligning with similar time 
windows observed in previous research
7. 
While prior studies have examined N2pc in subliminal cueing effects
10,30, they primarily focused on 
N2pc elicited by cues rather than targets. In our second experiment, we observed significant 
differences in target-elicited N2pc, indicating improved target attention facilitated by subliminal 
retro-cues. This component can be employed in subsequent studies on subliminal spatial cueing 
attention, particularly in research investigating subliminal retro-cueing effects. 
The controversy on P3b and VAN. 
In the realm of consciousness research, one of the most critical questions concerns the neural 
correlates of consciousness (NCC)
1,29,30. Previous studies have intensely debated whether P3b and 
VAN are neural correlates of consciousness
11,19,31,32. In classical report paradigms, P3b was 
considered a consciousness-related ERP component. However, recent no-report paradigm studies 
suggest that P3b may instead reflect motor preparation potentials related to task-specific button 
pressing, thereby excluding it as a candidate for NCC. Conversely, in a recent auditory study, 
Sergent et al. identified a new late negative component during the P3b time window under no-
report conditions, with its amplitude correlating with linear stimulus intensity increases. These 
divergent findings add complexity to the debate, and a recent preprint by Cohen proposed that 
N2 is the neural marker of consciousness
13. 
Under no-report conditions, our study produced results that contradict previous findings
6,13,18,19,30,32,33: 
within the 300–600 ms time window, the P3b component was still present under high-contrast

Based on these findings, we contend that prior conclusions regarding conscious perception 
derived from no-report paradigms warrant skepticism. Firstly, except for experiments 
incorporating eye-tracking measurements, previous studies could not ensure that participants

highlighted that eye movement patterns do not consistently and accurately reflect the content of

To address these concerns, we adopted Sergent et al.’s approach from auditory perception 
experiments by increasing the complexity of tasks unrelated to the stimuli

participants might still potentially falsify responses in mind-wandering detection questions). These

Our results challenge the simplistic exclusion of P3b from the neural correlates of consciousness

definitive neural markers of consciousness and highlights the necessity for more nuanced 
experimental designs that rigorously control for attention and perception. 
However, alternative interpretations of our experimental results remain possible: Complex Task 
Design and Mind-Wandering Detection: The intricate task design, along with the introduction of 
mind-wandering detection tasks, may have led participants to adopt covert report strategies. 
Frequency of Key Presses: The number of trials requiring key presses exceeded those that did not, 
resulting in participants predominantly employing motor preparation strategies in most trials. 
Consequently, motor preparation-related potentials were not fully excluded.

separate analyses of participants who performed the no-report task first did not reveal any 
significant differences compared to the overall results. Eliminating Motor Preparation-Related 
Potentials: In the no-report task, participants were instructed to make responses that were entirely 
unrelated to the visual stimuli, and the presentation order was appropriately balanced to avoid 
systematic biases. More importantly, the ERP amplitudes we present were derived by subtracting

Retrospective attention as a trigger for conscious perception 
To determine whether retrospective attention can indeed enhance conscious perception
4,6,8,11 
across a broader range of physical stimulation intensities, we conducted the third experiment. If 
no significant neural signature differences were observed between valid and invalid conditions in 
the no-report task, it would suggest that retrospective attention primarily enhances attention 
directed at the target rather than conscious perception. Conversely, an increase in the amplitude 
of the ERP component associated with conscious perception, or improved brain decoding 
performance and advanced decoding time for the target Gabor patch with post-cueing attention, 
regardless of reporting, would indicate enhanced conscious perception. 
The present results corroborate previous findings on the retrospective effects of attention on 
conscious perception observed by other researchers
4,8. Notably, the subjective visibility results 
demonstrated a significant post-cue effect across low, medium, and high contrast levels, a finding 
not paralleled by accuracy measurements in the orientation discrimination task. This discrepancy 
could be attributed to the subjective measurement's heightened sensitivity compared to the 
objective measurement, which may be susceptible to ceiling and floor effects. While prior studies 
primarily employed behavioral experiments to verify the effects on conscious perception, this study 
rigorously tested these effects at the neural level, particularly through multivariate pattern analysis.  
Multivariate pattern analysis, particularly temporal generalization analysis, has primarily been 
applied in conscious perception experiments involving linearly increasing physical intensities. 
However, Mathieu Landry and colleagues recently employed this method to discriminate between 
the neural signatures of exogenous and endogenous spatial cueing attention.
38 Their study 
elegantly demonstrated how multivariate pattern analysis could corroborate earlier observations 
made using behavioural and ERP measures. In the present study, we adopted a similar strategy to 
investigate the effects of retrospective attention at the neural level. A comparison of 12 different 
conditions confirmed that retrospective cueing attention can indeed improve the conscious 
perception of Gabor patches. The decoding performance revealed a distinct pattern, showing that 
the benefits of retro-cued attention were due to an enhancement of the neural signature of 
consciousness during later time courses (400-700 ms), as indicated by previous studies
13,23-28. (Fig 
9, 10, 11). 
These findings dismiss the hypothesis that the retrospective attention effect arises from memory 
rather than a perceptual effect. If retro-perception merely prevented the rapid decay of seen 
representations in memory, we would have observed no significant difference in neural signals 
between valid retro-cue trials and invalid or no-cue trials in the no-report task, since post-
perceptual processes were excluded in this design and no stimulus is required to be memorized. 
However, the data showed clear evidence of enhancement in neural signals. Contrary to the widely

The relationship between attention and consciousness 
Contemporary theories on the relationship between attention and consciousness propose three 
main perspectives
43: Double Dissociation between Attention and Consciousness
44: This view asserts 
that attention can modulate sensory processing even without conscious perception, while 
conscious experiences can and do occur without attention. Single Dissociation between Attention 
and Consciousness: This stance maintains that while attention can modulate sensory processing 
regardless of conscious perception, conscious awareness requires attention. In other words, 
attention and consciousness are distinct mental processes - attention can operate independently 
of consciousness, but not vice versa. Attention is necessary for consciousness. No Dissociation: 
The third perspective opposes any type of dissociation, proposing that consciousness is attention. 
The first perspective is supported by the Recurrent Processing Theory (RPT), while the last two are 
mainly advocated by the Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT). 
Our current research data supports the single dissociation perspective between attention and 
consciousness. In Experiments 1 and 2, we demonstrated that retrospective attention can 
modulate sensory processing under unconscious conditions. Furthermore, in Experiment 3, 
retrospective attention could still modulate conscious perception in high-contrast conditions even 
under no-report paradigms, indicating that attention continues to play a role in conscious 
perception even after controlling for decision-making and motor-related neural activities. 
Notably, we employed a retro-cueing spatial attention paradigm
45, which revealed that spatial 
attention can modulate consciousness in purely perceptual conditions even 67ms after target 
presentation. This demonstrates a highly flexible temporal window for attentional modulation of 
consciousness, providing compelling experimental evidence for the single dissociation perspective. 
 
Searchlight and neural correlates of consciousness 
The Searchlight Decoding Analysis in Experiment 3 revealed distinct neural patterns associated 
with the Report and No-report conditions under valid and invalid scenarios across varying Gabor 
contrasts. In the Report condition, the highest decoding performance clusters were consistently 
localized within the fronto-parietal regions, particularly under medium and high contrast 
conditions (Figure 10 A). This localization suggests that the fronto-parietal network, known for its 
role in attention and decision-making processes, is actively engaged when participants are 
required to report their perceptions, especially when the visual stimuli are more salient. In contrast, 
the No-report condition demonstrated peak decoding performance near the occipital areas, 
notably under high contrast conditions (Figure 10 A). This parieto-occipital dominance aligns with 
the primary visual processing functions of the occipital lobe, indicating that in the absence of a 
reporting task, neural activity remains more confined to sensory processing regions, consistent 
with previous research on consciousness
6,46-48. The shift from fronto-parietal to occipital regions 
between Report and No-report conditions highlights the modulatory effect of task demands on 
neural engagement. Specifically, the requirement to report appears to recruit higher-order

Although late components in ERP, time decoding, and time generalization analyses are capable

Limitations of the study 
It is noteworthy that in the no-report task, we only observed the retrospective attention effect in 
the high-contrast condition. This may be due to our use of an adapted Posner Cueing Paradigm
45, 
which is typically employed in spatial attention research rather than studies on consciousness. As 
a result, participants were required to divide their attention between two locations simultaneously. 
In the no-report task, however, participants were asked to perform a task entirely unrelated to the 
stimulus, thus allocating less attention to the target stimulus. Consequently, the contrast threshold 
for the Gabor patches may have increased. This is evident in the medium contrast condition, where 
the decoding performance in the invalid condition was higher than in the valid condition (Figs. 8, 
10) when attention was not fully focused on the Gabor task. In other words, the cue presented at 
the same location produced an interference effect—similar to a backward masking effect
14,15,37. 
Previous studies on the bifurcation model of consciousness have generally employed stimuli with 
the same physical intensity in both report and no-report tasks
17,18, and this study followed that 
approach as well. In the field of consciousness research, some researchers have used eye 
movement measurements
48-50 to ensure that physical stimuli are visible even when participants do 
not provide explicit reports. Therefore, in future studies on the retrospective spatial cueing effect, 
it would be advisable to include eye movement measurements to ensure that participants' 
subjective visibility in no-report tasks does not significantly differ from their visibility in report tasks. 
However, a more recent review
51 suggests that eye movement patterns do not always accurately 
reflect the content of conscious perception and can sometimes significantly diverge from it
6,42. 
Additionally, although we controlled for the sequence effects of the report and no-report tasks 
(see Methods), we did not specifically examine the potential impact of the order of these sessions, 
which has been rigorously tested in a previous study
12. Lastly, we did not investigate potential 
sex/gender differences in retrospective attention effects, which could be a valuable avenue for 
future research.

Building on our findings, future research should explore the incorporation of eye-tracking 
measures alongside retro-cueing paradigms to better ascertain the relationship between attention 
and conscious perception. Additionally, investigating sex/gender differences could provide deeper

From a practical standpoint, our findings have potential applications in areas such as attention

conscious perception at the neural level, distinguishing between sensory interaction and 
attentional mechanisms. By demonstrating that subliminal retro-cues modulate both N2pc and 
P3b components, we highlight the intricate relationship between attention and consciousness. Our 
findings support the single dissociation perspective, suggesting that while attention can operate 
independently, it is a necessary component for conscious awareness. These insights contribute to 
the ongoing discourse on the neural correlates of consciousness and theories of consciousness 
and pave the way for future investigations into the complex interplay between attention, 
perception, and consciousness.

METHOD DETAILS 
Stimuli 
In both the Experiments 1 and Experiments 2, the stimuli consisted of two horizontally arranged 
black circles (1.3 cd/m², 3° visual angle in diameter) on the screen, along with a central fixation 
cross (1.3 cd/m², 0.5° visual angle) for fixation. These circles were positioned 5° to the left and 
right of the fixation point. The experimental procedure was divided into a threshold testing 
phase and a formal experiment phase. Cues were briefly presented (16.67 ms) as a dimming (20 
cd/m²) of one of the two black circles, followed by a longer dimming (83.33 ms) of both circles 
(with brightness adjusted for each participant) to mask the cue in the Invisible Cue conditions. In 
the Visible Cue conditions, there was no dimming, and a fixation screen was shown instead. The 
target stimulus was a Gabor patch (2° visual angle in diameter) tilted either 30° or 150° from the 
vertical. In Experiments 3, the stimuli were similar to those in the Experiments 1 and Experiments 
2, with the following adjustments: the diameter of the black circles was increased to 4° visual 
angle, and the diameter of the Gabor patch was increased to 3° visual angle. Additionally, the 
two black circles were positioned 5° above and below the fixation point, rather than to the sides. 
 
Behavioral experiments Overview 
The experiment was programmed using Psychtoolbox based on Matlab2016b. All stimuli were 
presented on a 19-inch LED computer screen with a resolution of 1024×768 and a refresh rate 
of 60 Hz. The background was gray (28 cd/m²). 
In the Experiments 1, two horizontally arranged black circles (1.3 cd/m², 3° visual angle in 
diameter) were always present on the screen. In Experiments 2, the black circles on the left and 
right sides were shifted downward by 2.5° of visual angle compared to the Experiments 1. In 
Experiments 3, two vertically arranged black circles (1.3 cd/m², 4° visual angle in diameter) were 
used instead. A fixation cross (1.3 cd/m², 0.5° visual angle) was used for fixation. 
In the Experiments 1 and Experiments 2, the black circles were positioned 5° to the left and right 
of the fixation point. In Experiments 3, the black circles were positioned 5° above and below the 
fixation point. The viewing distance was 57 cm, and participants' head positions were stabilized 
using a chin rest. The black fixation cross was always displayed at the center of the screen.

orientation discrimination thresholds of the target Gabor using a 2AFC staircase procedure 
aimed at achieving 75% accuracy. This was done to ensure equal performance across both visible 
and invisible cue conditions. The stimuli and procedure for this threshold measurement task

participant were discarded. Baseline correction using the time window from -100 to 0ms pre-
stimulus. 
 
ERPs and cue, cue-mask subtraction 
Trials were sorted by condition and trial type (valid, invalid, or cue/cue-mask only). Each group of 
trials was averaged at the participant level and low-pass filtered at 25 Hz. To isolate stimulus-
related neural activity from cue/cue-mask-evoked activity, the cue/cue-mask-only ERPs were 
time-shifted to align the timing of the cues with cue onset in the stimulus-cue-mask trials, 
following the procedure of Del Cul and Cohen
13,17. This was done for each condition, and the time-
shifted mask ERPs were subtracted from the corresponding stimulus-cue-mask ERPs as follows: 
The cue/cue-mask-only ERP is initially time-locked to cue onset (i.e., time 0 is when the first cue 
was presented). 
The cue/cue-mask-only ERP is then shifted forward in time so that cue onset aligns with cue onset 
in each of the stimulus-cue-mask ERPs. 
The time-shifted mask ERPs are subtracted from the corresponding stimulus-cue-mask ERPs. 
 
ERP analysis 
Three ERPs of interest were identified based on data from the initial group: the N2pc in 
Experiments 2, the VAN in Experiments 3 (both displaying bifurcation dynamics in the no-report 
condition), and the P3b (displaying bifurcation dynamics in the report condition). The following 
time windows and electrodes were selected for each component and pre-registered on OSF 
(nearest channels of the international 10-20 system are reported in parentheses): 
N2pc: 240-280 ms, electrodes 9, 42, 56 (O1/O2, PO3/PO4, PO7/PO8). 
P3b: 300-600 ms, electrodes 1, 4-6, 13-15 (Pz, P1, P2, CPz, CP1, CP2, Cz). 
VAN: 240-280 ms, electrodes 9, 10, 42, 43, 56, 57 ( P5, P6, P7, P8, PO7, PO8) 
These time windows and electrodes were used for all analyses of these three ERPs. Additionally, a 
post-hoc analysis of the VAN was conducted, measured from 240-280 ms at electrodes 9, 10, 42, 
43, 56, 57 (P5, P6, P7, P8, PO7, PO8).

All decoding was conducted using the MVPA-Light toolbox
20 and fieldtrip
53 for MATLAB. Data was 
exported from BrainVision Analyzer and formatted for use with the MVPA-Light toolbox using 
EEGLAB. For each cue validity and task condition, a linear classifier (LDA) was trained to distinguish

remaining subset of trials. This process was repeated nine times, ensuring that the decoder was 
both trained and tested on the entire dataset over the course of the procedure. Importantly, the 
classifier was never tested on data used for training when assessing performance.

Temporal generalization 
The temporal generalization analysis technique characterizes the neural dynamics of perceptual 
representations by testing if a classifier trained on data from one time point can successfully 
discriminate between classes when applied to data from different time points. If a classifier 
trained at time T1 is able to decode stimulus presence at a level significantly above chance when 
tested at time T2, it suggests that the neural dynamics supporting above-chance decoding at T1 
are recurring at T2.  
Temporal generalization matrices were computed by training classifiers on one time point and 
testing each at every other time point. Classification sensitivity was statistically tested against 
chance and corrected for multiple comparisons using 1000-permutation cluster-based 
permutation testing.
