Abstract
Automation in complex industrial processes faces significant challenges due to its heavy reliance on human knowl­
edge and tacit expertise. This knowledge is typically acquired through years of on-site training, making the transition to 
automation difficult. While data-driven approaches offer potential solutions by building automation models from expert 
observations, they often fall short of capturing deeper human understanding and contextual reasoning. In this paper, we 
introduce Augmentor, a cognitive-inspired framework for context-aware adaptive learning. The framework bridges reac­
tive learning, which continuously updates models based on incoming data, with context-aware adaptive learning, which 
incorporates external context and expert knowledge for higher-order reasoning and decision-making. Inspired by theories 
of consciousness, Augmentor leverages autonomic computing principles through the MAPE-K model and semantic web 
technologies to create a global workspace for managing knowledge and guiding adaptive learning processes. The approach 
addresses two key objectives: continuously refining models through reactive learning and enabling experts to inject con­
textual knowledge to guide the learning process. Human-Gated Data Aggregation is used for data-driven updates, while 
context-aware adaptive learning ensures the system dynamically adjusts to complex scenarios and evolving industrial 
conditions. We validate Augmentor through a real-world use case in collaboration with our industrial partner, Mekanisk 
Service Halden, which specialises in various mechanical services. Experimental results show significant performance 
improvements across three strategies–static model without relearning, reactive learning, and context-aware adaptive learn­
ing–demonstrating the advantages of incorporating context-aware strategies to achieve more robust and adaptive control.

We present a practical use case in CNC manufacturing 
developed in collaboration with Mekanisk Service Halden 
(MSH),1 a company specializing in mechanical services 
such as welding, industrial maintenance, and parts machin­
ing. The case addresses the growing challenge of labor 
shortages in the CNC sector. Scaling up production requires 
skilled operators, but traditional training often takes 5 to 7 
years due to the tacit nature of machining expertise, which 
is acquired primarily through hands-on experience and is 
difficult to formalize. We collected sensor data from a real 
industrial CNC machine, Mazak Integrex i500 series. Aug­
mentor learns from experienced operators by observing their 
control strategies, such as adjustments to the linear feed rate 
override. By integrating adaptive policies into its knowledge 
base in real time, the system accelerates its learning process 
and improves its responsiveness. Our findings demonstrate 
that expert human guidance significantly enhances learning 
efficiency, reduces error rates, and improves system perfor­
mance. By capturing control patterns directly from opera­
tors, Augmentor aims to modernize traditional monitoring 
processes and support operators in overseeing multiple 
machines or focusing on higher-level tasks. Through skill-
level–adapted decision support, the system contributes to 
increased productivity, reduced production errors, and the 
development of a more resilient and capable workforce.

Imitation learning in human-centric systems
Imitation Learning (IL) is a machine learning technique 
designed for learning directly from human behavior (Hus­
sein et al., 2017). Instead of defining explicit rules or reward 
functions, IL enables a model to learn a task by observing 
and replicating demonstrations provided by human experts 
(Schaal, 1996). IL is particularly effective in domains where 
explicitly defining task objectives or reward functions is 
challenging, but expert behavior can be recorded. However, 
traditional IL often relies on static datasets, which limits its 
ability to adapt to new or unforeseen situations, leading to 
issues such as distributional shift, where the model encoun­
ters states not represented in the original demonstrations 
(Ross & Bagnell, 2010). A common solution to this prob­
lem is online learning to update learning policies continu­
ously. In imitation learning, this involves keeping experts 
in the loop to provide ongoing guidance and new informa­
tion. Active Learning improves data efficiency by involving 
humans in the labeling process more strategically. Instead 
of labeling all data, humans are asked to annotate only the

Learning from multiple experts
Learning from multiple experts has been extensively stud­
ied, particularly in the context of offline learning, where the 
entire dataset is available from the beginning. In these set­
tings, the learner can optimize its training process with full 
knowledge of the data. In contrast, online learning, where 
data arrives sequentially, presents additional challenges. 
One key difficulty is designing a no-regret algorithm, which 
aims to perform nearly as well as the best fixed strategy in 
hindsight, even without prior access to all the data. Algo­
rithms like DAgger must carefully evaluate the reliability of 
expert feedback against predefined safety criteria to avoid 
accumulating poor decisions over time. To address this, Sun 
et al. proposed Multiple-Expert-GAted DAgger (MEGA-
DAgger), which uses a Control Barrier Function as a gating 
mechanism to enforce safety constraints (Sun et al., 2024). 
Expert observations that violate these safety conditions are 
excluded from the aggregated dataset, preventing unsafe 
behaviors from being incorporated into the training process.
The approach used in MEGA-DAgger emphasizes the 
safety of critical systems but overlooks the nuances of 
human operators’ behaviors. Operational styles are far more 
varied and complex. A less assertive operational style may 
not be critical to system safety but could result in subopti­
mal production outcomes, such as longer machining times. 
While these observations are less valuable than those from 
more attentive or skillful operators, they still contribute 
valuable data to the learner model. However, the contribu­
tions from different operators should not be treated equally; 
instead, they should be weighted differently based on their 
impact and relevance to the learning process.
Autonomic computing
Autonomic computing, introduced by IBM in 2001, was 
proposed as a framework for developing self-managing and 
self-adaptive computer systems (Corporation, 2001). This 
paradigm draws inspiration from biological organisms, 
particularly the autonomic nervous system, which oper­
ates autonomously to manage and adapt to environmental 
changes. For instance, essential functions like heartbeat 
regulation and sweating are controlled seamlessly without 
conscious recognition or effort on the part of humans. IBM 
envisioned computer systems achieving a similar level of 
most informative samples selected by the model (Settles, 
2011). Interactive Machine Learning goes a step further by 
making the human an active collaborator in the learning 
process. Humans are not limited to labeling data. Instead, 
they can provide real-time corrections and feature guidance, 
which allow adjustments to the model’s behavior (Amershi 
et al., 2014).
Active Learning and Interactive Machine Learning pri­
marily involve human input in the form of labels or feed­
back, but do not typically allow humans to modify the 
learning algorithm itself (Mosqueira-Rey et al., 2023).
Dataset aggregation
A widely used interactive machine learning technique for 
addressing distributional shift in imitation learning is the 
Dataset Aggregation (DAgger) algorithm (Ross et al., 2011). 
DAgger reduces distributional shift by allowing the model 
to perform actions while a human expert provides correc­
tive labels for the states it encounters. These expert-labeled 
instances are incrementally added to the training dataset, 
allowing the policy to adapt and improve iteratively. By 
combining autonomous execution with continuous expert 
guidance, DAgger demonstrates how interactive learning 
can lead to more robust and adaptive models in dynamic, 
real-world environments.

training dataset. The meta-algorithm for determining the

weight decay, where the probability of using expert policies

Machine consciousness
Computational neuroscience and artificial intelligence 
(AI) have long been closely connected, sharing a common 
goal of understanding and replicating learning processes 
observed in the human brain. Computational neuroscien­
tists aim to decipher how the brain processes information, 
uncovering the mechanisms behind biological learning and 
memory. AI researchers then apply these insights to develop 
effective algorithms. For instance, convolutional neural net­
works (CNNs) for vision tasks (Fukushima, 1980), Lecun 
et al. (1998) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for 
sequential processing (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) 
draw inspiration from biological neural models, achieving 
remarkable success in their respective domains (Dong et al., 
2021). By exploring machine consciousness within cogni­
tive neuroscience, we aim to deepen our understanding of 
human learning processes, which can, in turn, be translated 
into advanced machine learning algorithms that foster a 
more nuanced model of human-augmented learning.
Cognitive processes
The definition of consciousness remains a topic of debate, as 
different fields interpret it uniquely from their own perspec­
tives. In computational neuroscience, one widely accepted 
definition involves the Global Workspace Theory (GWT) 
proposed by Baars (1988). GWT is a framework in cogni­
tive science for understanding consciousness. It suggests 
that consciousness emerges when information is broadcast 
across a centralized "global workspace" in the brain. This 
workspace enables the integration of information from vari­
ous unconscious processes–such as perception, memory, 
and reasoning, making it simultaneously accessible to 
multiple brain regions. Under this framework, information 
processing is divided into two categories: unconscious and 
conscious processing.
	
●
Unconscious Processing Many complex mental tasks, 
such as recognizing faces or understanding speech, are 
carried out automatically by the brain without conscious 
awareness. When the output of such a process becomes 
strong or relevant enough, it crosses a threshold and 
enters the global workspace. This transition transforms 
the previously unconscious activity into a conscious 
process, allowing it to be accessed and used by other 
cognitive functions (Dehaene et al., 2003), Mashour et 
al. (2020).
	
●
Conscious Processing Information is considered con­
scious when it becomes globally accessible–that is when 
it can be shared across various cognitive systems such 
as memory, attention, language, and decision-making.

their own environments.
To realize this vision, IBM proposed a control loop com­
posed of five key building blocks: Monitoring, Analysing, 
Planning, Execution, and Knowledge. IBM’s MAPE-K 
model and their vision of autonomic computing systems 
did not follow the conceptual model of machine conscious­
ness, thus it does not differentiate between conscious and 
unconscious processes. Previous studies have proposed 
incorporating learning processes, such as reinforcement 
learning, into the MAPE-K framework. For instance, Tes­
auro suggested integrating reinforcement learning as a core 
component of the MAPE loop, enhancing each phase with 
a dynamic, learning-based approach to self-management 
(Tesauro, 2007). However, this approach constrained the 
MAPE loop to address specific functionalities within the 
system, rather than integrating knowledge and information 
across multiple processes or functionalities. 
1.	 Monitoring-block collects information from external 
sources, such as sensors, and internal sources, including 
reactive processes, to provide a comprehensive view of 
the system’s state.
2.	 Analysing-block processes the data received from the 
Monitor Block to generate actionable insights and a 
deeper understanding of the environment.
3.	 Planning-block uses the knowledge constructed by the 
Analyze Block and its own understanding of the envi­
ronment to develop rational strategies, guiding the sys­
tem toward optimal performance.
4.	 Execution-block implements the strategies devised by 
the Plan Block by disseminating information to the 
appropriate sub-processes for execution.
5.	 Knowledge-blocks serves as a repository for storing 
information, enabling future iterations of the MAPE-K 
loop to leverage past experiences. It facilitates the cre­
ation of new knowledge, which influences other blocks 
to adapt and refine their behavior over time.
Machine learning has been introduced several times in the 
past. One of the earliest attempts was to transform the entire 
MAPE-K loop into a reinforcement learning loop (Tesauro, 
2007). Machine learning is commonly used to enhance 
various components of the MAPE-K loop in self-adaptive 
systems (Gheibi et al., 2021). It supports monitoring by 
improving data interpretation, analysis through prediction 
and anomaly detection, planning by optimizing adaptation 
strategies, and, in rare cases, execution by selecting appro­
priate actions. The choice of where ML is applied depends 
on the system’s goals and the nature of the adaptation 
problem.

Learning theory
Learning is the process of acquiring new knowledge through 
experiences. Evidence of learning is typically demonstrated 
by a change in behavior. However, behavior changes can 
be caused by other factors, like system disruption. To dif­
ferentiate, the learning process is specified as changes in 
behavior through experiences (Lefrancois, 2019). Research­
ers from different disciplines might have different theories 
for learning. Still, they all agree that learning is incremental 
and iterative, which means that knowledge is gathered and 
accumulated via repeating cycles.
The learning process happens at both unconscious and 
conscious levels, each approaches in different ways to 
acquire and refine knowledge and skills.
	
●
In the unconscious level, learning is characterized as 
an automatic and background process. The brain pas­
sively absorbs patterns, updating its internal models 
without conscious intervention. Some of the automatic 
learning models that have been agreed upon across dif­
ferent disciplines include subliminal priming (Kouider 
& Dehaene, 2007), reinforcement learning (Thorndike, 
1933), and latent learning (Lefrancois, 2019), Tolman 
and Honzik (1930).
Conscious processing typically involves active regula­
tion, requiring deliberate attention, focused effort, or in­
tentional choices beyond automatic responses. Equally 
essential is the second dimension of consciousness: 
self-awareness (Dehaene et al., 2017). This allows us 
to monitor and reflect on our own thoughts, recall past 
experiences, and engage in higher-level reasoning and 
complex problem-solving.
Building upon GWT, the Global Neuronal Workspace 
(GNW) model offers a more neuroscience-grounded per­
spective, suggesting that certain brain regions, particularly 
the prefrontal and parietal cortices, form a network that 
facilitates conscious processing (Dehaene et al., 1998), 
Mashour et al. (2020). The GNW highlights how specific 
brain areas act as hubs for integrating sensory and cogni­
tive information, leading to the emergence of conscious 
awareness (Fig. 3). This concept has significant implica­
tions for AI research, as it provides a model for creating 
systems capable of managing and integrating vast amounts 
of information while prioritizing relevant data, ultimately 
moving AI closer to more human-like awareness and cogni­
tive flexibility.

Reactive learning
This section addresses Objective 1 by introducing a con­
tinuous learning algorithm. The algorithm incrementally 
refines the behavioral cloning models developed in our pre­
vious work, where models were trained on a fixed dataset 
(Nguyen et al., 2023). This approach allows refinement by 
observing expert behavior in real-time.
Definition 1  Reactive learning refers to a continuous, data-
driven process in which learning algorithms automatically 
update their parameters based on incoming data, triggered 
by predefined conditions.
DAgger and other online machine learning algorithms 
are examples of reactive learning algorithms because 
they respond to predefined triggers. These triggers can be 
condition-based, such as a confidence threshold in DAg­
ger, which reflects how closely the algorithm’s predictions 
match the expert policy. Alternatively, reactive learning 
can also be continuous, where the trigger is a predefined 
number of iterations or the sampling strategy. In DAgger, 
this involves alternating between the expert policy and 
the current learned policy, with learning triggered periodi­
cally as the system collects new samples according to the 
implementation strategy. In manufacturing environments, 
data is gathered from a broad range of experts rather than 
a select few. This approach offers the benefit of capturing a 
diverse array of experiences and scenarios, making data col­
lection easier and reducing the risk of distributional shifts. 
However, a significant challenge arises from the variabil­
ity in operational styles among different operators, shaped 
by their personal preferences and production needs. For 
instance, within the same production environment, some 
operators might adopt a cautious approach, slowing down 
machine speeds in advance to minimize risks, while oth­
ers may take a more active approach, making real-time 
adjustments based on situational demands. Each style has 
its own advantages and drawbacks. The cautious approach 
helps preserve tools, reduces workload, and provides peace 
of mind for operators but at the cost of slower production 
speeds. On the other hand, the active approach optimizes 
production speed and maintains tool efficiency but demands 
greater attention and skill from operators. These human fac­
tors significantly influence the data collected, presenting 
challenges for machine learning algorithms to discern opti­
mal patterns from the varied evidence available.

Conscious learning reflects active, intentional learning. 
The process requires focus, attention, and mental effort 
(Dehaene et al., 2017). Active learning is an integral 
part of consciousness. It requires global accessibility of 
knowledge, information filtering, and memory recall. 
By reflecting on the information via abstract thinking 
and complex reasoning, it facilitates the creation of new 
knowledge, more nuanced and deeper. Conscious learn­
ing also includes meta-cognitive reflection allows our 
own learning process to identify errors, adjust strategies, 
and refine our approach based on this self-assessment.
The majority of human knowledge operates at the uncon­
scious level (Baars, 1997), making it inherently difficult to 
formalize or articulate explicitly. This form of knowledge 
was first described as “tacit knowledge” by Michael Polanyi 
in 1966 (Polanyi, 1966). According to Baars, while actual 
encoding and processing of patterns happens implicitly, 
conscious awareness is required to trigger learning and the 
selection of learning materials (Baars, 1997).
The reinforcement learning mechanism in machine learn­
ing is the direct inspiration of its counterpart in biology. In 
this paradigm, agents interact with an environment, making 
decisions to maximize cumulative rewards over time (Sut­
ton & Barto, 1998). Bayesian learning is a computational 
framework based on Bayes’ Theorem, which provides a 
principled way to update beliefs in light of new evidence 
(MacKay, 2002). Though not a direct model of biological

Experiment
The experiment was designed to validate the two main 
objectives of developing a context-aware adaptive learning 
mechanism.
Data preparation
For our experiment, we collected data from the Mazak Inte­
grex i500 at MSH over four months, spanning October 2023 
to January 2024. During this period, the machine produced 
multiple product series.
Definition 3  An episode refers to the period during which 
the CNC machine produces a unit of the product design. 
This concept is adapted from reinforcement learning, where 
an episode represents a complete sequence of interactions 
between an agent and its environment, beginning in an ini­
tial state and concluding in a terminal state (Sutton & Barto, 
1998).
A chuck is a tool that holds and rotates the workpiece dur­
ing machining. The machine operates only when the chuck 
is in the CLOSED state. We used the ChuckState signal to 
divide the sensor data stream into separate episodes. When 
the ChuckState transitions from OPEN to CLOSED, 
it marks the start of an episode. Conversely, when the 
ChuckState transitions from CLOSED to OPENED, it sig­
nifies the end of the episode, as the operator removes the 
finished part from the chuck grip. Episodes are grouped into 
a series if they share the same sequence of tool numbers and 
their timestamps are within a close interval, within one day 
or one week.
Definition 2  Context-aware adaptive learning involves 
global accessibility of knowledge, integrating both reac­
tive learning and contextual information. It requires self-
reflective properties to derive new knowledge that actively 
enhances the reactive learning process.
We leverage the conceptual model of autonomic comput­
ing to design our computational global workspace. While 
Baars (1988) provided a theoretical framework explaining 
brain function through the division of unconscious and sub­
conscious processes, IBM’s autonomic computing can be 
utilized to translate the concept into a computational frame­
work and outline how such systems could be realized in 
computer architecture. We build upon these foundations by 
integrating concepts from machine consciousness with tech­
nical frameworks from autonomic computing and machine 
learning to create a more nuanced version of the MAPE-K 
loop. In our design, computational processes are classified 
as follows: 
1.	 Reactive processes These include learning models 
driven by data, such as online learning, pattern recogni­
tion, and other data-driven mechanisms.
2.	 Context-aware adaptive Processes These occur within 
the MAPE-K loop and actively influence reactive pro­
cesses through the Execute block, ensuring adaptive 
and context-aware interactions.
Policies in the system can be created through two approaches: 
human-defined policies, where operators inject explicit pol­
icies based on their expertise and operational knowledge to 
guide system adaptation, and synthesized policies, where 
the system autonomously generates policies by analyzing 
accumulated evidence and contextual data, enhancing reac­
tive processes with learned adaptations.
The MAPE-K in the Augmentor system becomes the 
global workspace for context-aware adaptive processes. 
The MAPE-K loop itself provides a mechanism for global 
accessibility between reactive processes. While the Anal­
yse and Plan block utilizes information from the Knowl­
edge storage to achieve the self-awareness dimension of 
machine consciousness (Fig. 4). SPARQL and semantic 
web technologies are used to implement adaptation policies 
within MAPE-K. Unlike previous approaches to integrat­
ing machine learning into MAPE-K (Gheibi et al., 2021), 
our design treats learning algorithms as distinct reactive 
processes that report their outputs to the global workspace 
component of the MAPE-K loop. This separation of con­
cerns enables modular scalability, enabling the integration 
of multiple models and processes seamlessly.

Reactive learning processes
The learning model follows our enhanced HG-DAgger 
algorithm in Sect. 4.1. It starts with a high temperature T0 
and a high probability of accepting both high and low-con­
fidence samples. As temperature decreases, high-confidence 
samples are rarely accepted, but low-confidence samples are 
always accepted. The model focuses more on refining its 
understanding of the control patterns presented in the time 
series data. One key performance metric in CNC production 
is active time, with shorter durations being preferable. As 
a result, data from episodes with shorter active times are 
assigned higher sample weights compared to those with lon­
ger production times within the same series.
Our DAgger algorithm exhibits elements of subliminal 
priming learning. It passively absorbs information from 
stimuli when the mini-batch dataset becomes available for 
updating the model. In the absence of contextual input from 
the global workspace, DAgger operates according to its 
default algorithm, with sample weights maintaining their 
standard value of one.

A cutting path is defined as the interval beginning when 
the cutting tools engage the workpiece and ending when 
they lift and rapidly advance to the next cutting position. 
During the movement to the next position, the load on 
the turning spindle drops almost to zero, while movement 
speed and feed rates spike briefly. These intervals are con­
sidered transient periods, which can increase the variance 
of our dataset. We removed these segments from the train­
ing dataset. Identifying sudden shifts in time series behavior 
is known as change point detection. For this, we applied 
the PELT (Pruned Exact Linear Time) algorithm from the 
ruptures library (Truong et al., 2020). PELT detects an 
unknown number of change points, assuming they occur 
linearly (Killick et al., 2012). Abrupt changes in spindle 
load can also result from irregular surfaces. To differentiate 
between cutting paths and transient periods, segments with 
a quantile below ten between change points are labeled as 
transient and discarded (Fig. 5)

Discussion
Regarding the main objectives
Objective 1 aimed to continuously refine the imitation learn­
ing algorithm by learning from operators’ actions in real-
time. This was achieved through the enhanced HG-DAgger 
algorithm, which incrementally updated behavioral cloning 
suggesting that the knowledge gained by the learning algo­
rithm is progressively contributing to improved perfor­
mance in subsequent series. Transitioning from one series to 
another requires adaptation, leading to a temporary increase 
in growth rates (Fig. 13). Nonetheless, Fig. 14 shows a 
broader downward trend in regret across series, with later 
series exhibiting lower regret growth rates compared to ear­
lier ones. This demonstrates the learning model’s gradual 
improvement in adapting to new series over time.
The key difference between the reactive learner and the 
adaptive learner lies in the incorporation of additional con­
textual knowledge, which introduces bias or information 
gain. This contextual knowledge can take the form of rules 
or policies derived from human understanding or inferences

Challenges and future directions
This study concentrates on offline imitation learning. 
While it doesn’t offer the flexibility of interactive, real-
time approaches, it comes with clear benefits–especially 
in terms of safety, repeatability, and ease of deployment. 
These strengths make it well-suited for situations where live 
system access is limited or where safety concerns prevent 
direct experimentation. In future work, we plan to bring 
these algorithms into real-world deployment, allowing them 
to actively influence the system’s behavior and adapt based 
on real-time feedback.
This study concentrated on one specific operator task: 
adjusting the override feed rate. In practice, however, CNC 
operators are responsible for a range of other activities, such 
as changing tools, clearing chips, and responding to unex­
pected events. These tasks often require operators to rely on 
multiple sources of information, including what they hear 
and see during machine operation. Expanding this work 
to better capture the full scope of operator behavior would 
therefore require incorporating multimodal data. Recent 
models based on incoming data and expert feedback. The 
results show that the reactive learner, when compared to the 
static model, significantly reduced cumulative regret over 
time. This demonstrates that the model effectively assimi­
lated operator behavior and refined its control policies 
through repeated exposure and feedback. The staircase pat­
tern in regret across episodes further confirms this learning 
dynamic–each new series initially incurred high regret that 
gradually decreased as the model adapted, illustrating how 
learning accumulated and improved through experience.
Objective 2 focused on incorporating contextual knowl­
edge, environmental understanding, and operator insight into 
the learning system. This was addressed through the con­
text-aware adaptive learning framework, which augmented 
reactive learning with information broadcast through a cen­
tralized global workspace based on the MAPE-K architec­
ture. Unlike the reactive model, the context-aware model 
was able to selectively filter irrelevant or noisy data dur­
ing machine inactivity or transient states, and dynamically 
adjust sample weights based on contextual relevance. These 
context-sensitive adaptations led to a further reduction in 
regret compared to the reactive learner alone, validating the 
effectiveness of integrating high-level expert knowledge 
and reasoning into the learning loop.
Together, these results demonstrate that the Augmen­
tor system meets both objectives–reactively learning from 
expert demonstrations and adaptively improving through 
structured, context-aware reasoning.
Actionable insights
The study focused on a specific form of tacit knowledge 
involved in controlling feed rate. It showed how this type 
of knowledge can be modeled as a reactive, data-driven 
process within Augmentor which is functionally equiva­
lent to unconscious learning in Baar’s Global Workspace 
Theory (GWT) (Baars, 1988). In line with Baars’ theory 
(Baars, 1997), the triggering of learning and the reception 
of relevant input through the global workspace reflect how 
conscious access guides what is learned, while the actual 
processing remains unconscious.
The experimental results not only validate the effective­
ness of the proposed learning framework but also offer sev­
eral insights that can inform the design of future adaptive 
systems:
	
●
Inspired by the Global Workspace Theory, this study 
shows that learning processes can be organized as modu­
lar, reactive agents whose outputs are selectively broad­
cast and coordinated through a shared global workspace. 
This architecture facilitates learning across processes, 
enabling multimodal integration and information reuse,

1.	 Reactive processes: These include data-driven algo­
rithms such as learning models and pattern recognition 
techniques. Their primary function is to refine model 
parameters and adapt to new information embedded in 
the data.
2.	 Context-aware adaptive processes: These processes 
manage and integrate contextual information through 
the global workspace. They ensure global accessibility 
and facilitate the formation of new knowledge through 
human guidance and knowledge injection, enhancing 
the system’s adaptability and decision-making.
We demonstrated the application of the Augmentor frame­
work through an industrial use case focused on digitizing 
tacit knowledge from human operators. The system achieves 
two key objectives: 
1.	 Continuous updates to learning algorithms with new 
evidence from expert interventions: To meet this objec­
tive, we proposed an enhanced version of the DAgger 
algorithm that incorporates global accessibility to the 
global workspace, facilitating the fulfillment of the sec­
ond objective.
2.	 Integration of contextual knowledge into the learning 
system: Leveraging the autonomic computing approach 
with MAPE-K, we created a global workspace capa­
ble of broadcasting information from various reactive 
processes into the learning algorithm. The knowledge 
base within this workspace enables knowledge injec­
tion from experts and the generation of new contextual 
knowledge using semantic web technologies.
Our experimental results demonstrate that the Augmentor 
framework with context-aware adaptive learning signifi­
cantly outperforms traditional reactive DAgger, with the 
performance improvement equal to the knowledge bias 
introduced by expert contributions to the system.
Beyond the quantitative improvements, our study offers 
several actionable insights for the design of human-centered 
AI systems. Drawing inspiration from cognitive theory, we 
demonstrate how structuring learning architectures around 
concepts like global accessibility and modular process 
coordination enables more flexible and scalable adapta­
tion. Context-aware learning further enhances adaptability 
by softening the rigidity of traditional online algorithms, 
allowing them to filter, prioritize, and integrate informa­
tion based on operational relevance and expert input. 
Most importantly, by including humans in the learning 
loop–not as passive data providers but as active collabora­
tors–the system fosters a cooperative model of continuous 
co-evolution, where both humans and machines improve 
together. These findings suggest that AI systems grounded 
developments in sensing hardware and multimodal large 
language models (MLLMs) (Zhang et al., 2024) make it 
increasingly feasible to gather and interpret data from vari­
ous channels, such as sound, video, and temperature read­
ings. While this study relied on manually defined SPARQL 
rules to represent contextual information, MLLMs offer a 
compelling alternative to the traditional knowledge base in 
the MAPE-K loop, which serves as the core of the global 
workspace. MLLMs have the potential to automatically 
derive contextual rules from unstructured sources such as 
maintenance logs and operator notes, while maintaining 
semantic alignment with sensor data and supporting consis­
tent, context-aware reasoning. However, these models still 
lack reliable checking mechanism to ensure the accuracy 
and safety of the knowledge they generate (Chen & Shu, 
2024). The primary challenge in implementing the MAPE-
K framework as a global workspace lies in constructing the 
internal knowledge base, particularly in managing domain-
specific knowledge, which still requires the guidance of 
domain experts.
Our industry partner, MSH, has pointed to the increas­
ing shortage of skilled CNC operators as a pressing chal­
lenge. One proposed solution is to allow a single operator 
to supervise multiple machines at the same time. To support 
this kind of scalability, the system would need to adopt a 
multi-agent design, where each Augmentor unit can make 
decisions independently while still coordinating through 
a shared global workspace. Implementing this would 
involve complex scheduling and possibly planning strate­
gies (Wooldridge, 2009) to manage decision-making across 
machines, or even across multiple factory locations. Fur­
thermore, incorporating data on energy consumption and 
tool wear into the framework would make it more useful for 
addressing goals related to sustainability and cost control, 
which are becoming more important in modern manufactur­
ing environments.
By addressing these challenges and leveraging the 
strengths of modularity, formal knowledge representation, 
and adaptive learning, the outlined framework holds signifi­
cant potential for generalization across diverse and complex 
domains.

integration of contextual knowledge into online learning
